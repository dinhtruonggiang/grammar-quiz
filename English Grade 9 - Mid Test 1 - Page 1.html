Vocabulary and Grammar Questions

5. Teenagers who regularly consume fast food are at a higher _____________ of becoming obese.

Correct answer: D. likelihood

The correct collocation is "higher likelihood." Let's analyze each option:

  • "threat" - refers to an indication of something unpleasant coming; not used with "higher" in this way
  • "fear" - refers to being afraid; not appropriate in this context
  • "risk" - could work grammatically, but typically paired with "greater/increased risk" rather than "higher risk"
  • "likelihood" - fits perfectly with "higher" to indicate increased probability or chance
"Higher likelihood" is the most natural collocation to express increased probability of becoming obese.

6. Making healthy choices can help you _____________ a healthy weight.

Correct answer: B. maintain

The correct collocation is "maintain a healthy weight." Let's analyze each option:

  • "watch" - you can "watch your weight" but not "watch a healthy weight"
  • "maintain" - means to keep something at the same level or standard, which is exactly what we want to do with a healthy weight
  • "support" - not typically used with "weight" in this context
  • "succeed" - requires a preposition after it (succeed in/at something); not used directly with "a healthy weight"
"Maintain a healthy weight" is a common health-related expression and the most appropriate choice.

7. I need to _____________ a nap after lunch, or I won't have enough energy for the rest of the day.

Correct answer: A. take

The correct collocation is "take a nap." Let's analyze each option:

  • "take a nap" - this is the standard collocation in English for having a short sleep
  • "use a nap" - not a natural collocation in English
  • "spend a nap" - we spend time, not a nap
  • "give a nap" - might imply giving someone else a nap, not taking one yourself
"Take a nap" is the fixed expression used to describe having a short sleep during the day.

8. In some parts of the world, many people don't have access _____________ clean water and food.

Correct answer: D. to

The correct preposition to use with "access" is "to." Let's analyze each option:

  • "access for" - not grammatically correct with "access"
  • "access with" - not grammatically correct with "access"
  • "access on" - not grammatically correct with "access"
  • "access to" - this is the correct preposition to use with the noun "access"
"Access to" is a fixed collocation in English. We always say "have access to something" when talking about the availability of resources.

9. _____________ my sister has a gym membership, she rarely goes to the gym.

Correct answer: A. Despite

Let's analyze each option:

  • "Despite" - indicates contrast or contradiction, which fits the context (having a membership but not using it)
  • "Though" - typically used as "Although" at the beginning of a sentence, which would also work but is not offered as an option
  • "Since" - indicates a reason or time, which doesn't fit the context
  • "Due to" - indicates a reason or cause, which doesn't fit the context
"Despite" correctly expresses the contradiction between having a gym membership and rarely using it. Note that we would need either "Despite the fact that my sister has..." or "Despite having..." to be grammatically correct, but "Despite" is the best option among the choices given.

10. _____________ having a busy schedule, my mother always makes time to cook healthy meals for us.

Correct answer: A. In spite of

Let's analyze each option:

  • "In spite of" - expresses contrast and is followed by a noun or gerund, which fits perfectly here
  • "Although" - requires a subject-verb structure, not a gerund phrase; it would need to be "Although she has a busy schedule"
  • "Because" - indicates reason, not contrast, so it doesn't fit the meaning
  • "When" - indicates time, not contrast, so it doesn't fit the meaning
"In spite of having a busy schedule" correctly expresses the contrast between being busy and still finding time to cook healthy meals.

11. Some diets might suggest _____________ special pills or drinks that promise to help you lose weight.

Correct answer: C. taking

The verb "suggest" can be followed by different patterns. Let's analyze each option:

  • "suggest to take" - not a correct pattern with "suggest"
  • "suggest you took" - incorrect tense; we wouldn't use past tense here
  • "suggest taking" - correct pattern; "suggest" can be followed by a gerund
  • "suggest you'll take" - incorrect tense; we wouldn't use future tense with "suggest"
"Suggest taking" is grammatically correct. After "suggest," we can use either a gerund (verb + ing) or a that-clause, but not an infinitive. The gerund form "taking" is the appropriate choice here.

12. _____________ the strict diet that she has followed, she hasn't lost any weight.

Correct answer: D. Despite

Let's analyze each option:

  • "While" - typically introduces a time relationship or contrast with a clause, not a noun phrase
  • "Even though" - requires a subject-verb structure, would need to be "Even though she has followed a strict diet"
  • "Because" - indicates reason, which doesn't match the contrasting relationship in the sentence
  • "Despite" - correctly introduces a contrast and can be followed by a noun phrase
"Despite the strict diet" correctly expresses the contrast between following a strict diet and not losing weight. "Despite" is followed by a noun phrase, which fits the structure of the sentence.

Dialogue Completion

13. Elsa: "Why not take the stairs instead of the elevator? It's just two floors up."
Jack: "______________. It's good exercise."

Correct answer: A. Let's do it!

This is a dialogue where Elsa suggests taking the stairs instead of the elevator, and Jack needs to respond. Let's analyze each option:

  • "Let's do it!" - expresses agreement and enthusiasm, which logically connects to his following statement "It's good exercise"
  • "I don't think so." - expresses disagreement, which contradicts his following statement about exercise being good
  • "Why should I do it?" - expresses resistance or reluctance, which contradicts his following positive statement
  • "You can do it." - suggests only Elsa should take the stairs, not both of them, which doesn't fit with his following statement
"Let's do it!" shows Jack agreeing with Elsa's suggestion and is consistent with his comment about exercise being good.

14. Lizzy: "I think we should sign up for a cooking class."
Mandy: "______________."

Correct answer: C. That's a great idea.

This is a dialogue where Lizzy suggests signing up for a cooking class, and Mandy needs to respond. Let's analyze each option:

  • "Yes, you should." - suggests only Lizzy should sign up, not both of them, which doesn't fully respond to the suggestion
  • "Home-cooked meals are good." - states a fact but doesn't directly respond to the suggestion about taking a class
  • "That's a great idea." - directly responds to the suggestion with enthusiasm and agreement
  • "There are many cooking classes available." - provides information but doesn't indicate whether Mandy agrees with the suggestion
"That's a great idea" is the most appropriate response as it directly addresses Lizzy's suggestion with a clear expression of agreement.

15. What does the sign mean?

Correct answer: D. This parking space is only for visitors.

The sign "VISITOR PARKING ONLY" means that only visitors are allowed to park in this area. Let's analyze each option:

  • "Anyone can park their car here." - Incorrect, as the sign restricts parking to visitors only, not anyone
  • "You can park your car here unless you are visitors." - This is the opposite of what the sign means
  • "People can only park their car here after their visit." - Incorrect, as the sign indicates parking is for visitors, not specifically after visiting
  • "This parking space is only for visitors." - Correct interpretation of the sign
The sign is designating a specific area for visitor parking only, meaning if you're not a visitor, you're not supposed to park there.

16. What does the notice mean?

Correct answer: A. People can have more apple juice at the same price.

The notice "APPLE JUICE 10% EXTRA FREE" is a marketing promotion. Let's analyze each option:

  • "People can have more apple juice at the same price." - Correct. The promotion offers 10% more product for the same price
  • "People can have free apple juice." - Incorrect. The juice isn't free; there's just 10% extra volume included
  • "There is 10% of extra apple in this juice." - Incorrect. The promotion is about the volume of juice, not the apple content
  • "The apple juice is 10% free." - Unclear and incorrect interpretation
This is a common marketing promotion where the consumer gets 10% more product (more volume of juice) without paying extra for it.

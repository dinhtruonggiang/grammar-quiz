4. Which word has a different stress pattern from that of the others?

5. Sometimes, people are confused about what type of food is healthy and which one can be harmful _______ our health.

Correct answer: B. to

The correct preposition to use after "harmful" is "to". We say something is "harmful to [someone/something]". The other prepositions don't collocate correctly with "harmful" in this context:

  • "harmful with our health" - incorrect collocation
  • "harmful to our health" - correct collocation
  • "harmful for our health" - not the standard collocation
  • "harmful about our health" - incorrect collocation

6. Schools need to improve the menus of their _______ so that there are healthier options.

Correct answer: C. cafeteria

A "cafeteria" is the most appropriate word here because it refers to the dining facility within a school where students eat. The other options are less appropriate:

  • "restaurant" - typically refers to commercial establishments outside of schools
  • "market" - refers to a place where goods are bought and sold, not typically a place for prepared meals in schools
  • "buffet" - refers to a style of meal service rather than a location within a school
"Cafeteria" is the standard term for a dining facility within an educational institution.

7. Don't eat too much junk food, or you will _______ weight.

Correct answer: A. gain

In the context of eating too much junk food, the logical consequence would be to "gain weight". While "increase" has a similar meaning, "gain" is the standard collocation with "weight" in this context.

  • "gain weight" - correct collocation for adding body mass
  • "lose weight" - opposite of what would happen if eating too much junk food
  • "maintain weight" - would mean staying at the same weight, not what typically happens with excessive junk food
  • "increase weight" - grammatically possible but "gain weight" is the standard expression

8. Parents are _______ to limit children's television watching and computer use to less than 2 hours daily.

Correct answer: D. advised

The most appropriate word here is "advised" because it suggests receiving a recommendation from experts (like doctors or educators). The passive voice "are advised" indicates that parents are receiving this guidance from an authoritative source.

  • "are suggested" - not grammatically correct in this passive construction
  • "are promoted" - doesn't fit the context of giving recommendations to parents
  • "are insisted" - grammatically incorrect; "insist" requires "on" in passive voice
  • "are advised" - correct usage for receiving recommendations

9. Our teacher advised us _______ enough sleep before the exam.

Correct answer: B. to get

After the verb "advise", we typically use the infinitive "to + verb". In this case, "advised us to get" is the correct form.

  • "advised us getting" - incorrect structure; "advise" doesn't take a gerund object
  • "advised us to get" - correct structure with infinitive
  • "advised us get" - missing the "to" for the infinitive
  • "advised us got" - incorrect tense
The verb "advise" is typically followed by the infinitive form "to + base verb" when suggesting an action.

10. Doctors always suggest _______ exercise at least three times a week.

Correct answer: D. doing

After the verb "suggest", we typically use a gerund (verb + ing). The correct form here is "suggest doing".

  • "suggest us do" - incorrect structure; "suggest" doesn't take this pattern
  • "suggest us doing" - incorrect; "suggest" doesn't take an indirect object before the gerund
  • "suggest to do" - incorrect; "suggest" doesn't take an infinitive
  • "suggest doing" - correct structure with gerund
Unlike "advise" in the previous question, "suggest" is typically followed by a gerund (verb + ing) when recommending an action.

11. _______ teenagers are often aware of the importance of healthy eating, they may still make unhealthy choices.

Correct answer: A. Although

This sentence expresses a contrast between teenagers' awareness of healthy eating and their actual choices. "Although" is a subordinating conjunction that introduces a clause expressing a contrast.

  • "Although" - correctly expresses contrast between awareness and behavior
  • "Because" - would indicate that teenagers make unhealthy choices due to their awareness, which doesn't make logical sense
  • "When" - would suggest that teenagers only make unhealthy choices at times they're aware of healthy eating, which doesn't fit the meaning
  • "However" - typically connects two separate sentences, not clauses within one sentence
"Although" is appropriate for indicating the contradiction between awareness and action.

12. You shouldn't eat too much fried food _______ its delicious taste.

Correct answer: C. despite

The sentence indicates that even though fried food tastes delicious, you shouldn't eat too much of it. "Despite" best expresses this contrasting relationship.

  • "due to" - would suggest the delicious taste is the reason you shouldn't eat it, which is illogical
  • "instead of" - would suggest replacing the delicious taste with something else, which doesn't make sense
  • "despite" - correctly shows the contrast between the advice (don't eat too much) and the temptation (delicious taste)
  • "though" - grammatically incorrect in this position; would need to be "even though" or used differently
"Despite" means "regardless of" or "even though something exists", which fits the context perfectly.

13. Antony: "I think we really need to limit fried foods. They're not good for our cholesterol." David: "_______. I'll try to cut back."

Correct answer: B. You are right

In this dialogue, David is agreeing with Antony's statement about limiting fried foods, as evidenced by his follow-up "I'll try to cut back."

  • "It won't hurt" - doesn't clearly express agreement with Antony's concern
  • "You are right" - clearly shows agreement with Antony's statement
  • "I don't think so" - expresses disagreement, which contradicts "I'll try to cut back"
  • "Fair enough" - while this could work, it's less direct than "You are right" and suggests reluctant acceptance rather than clear agreement
"You are right" is the most appropriate response showing David's agreement with Antony's suggestion.

14. Cathy: "I'm thinking about cutting out all carbs to lose weight faster." Susan: "_______. Your body needs carbs for energy."

Correct answer: C. I don't think it's a good idea

In this dialogue, Susan is disagreeing with Cathy's plan to cut out all carbs, as shown by her explanation "Your body needs carbs for energy."

  • "I can't agree with you more" - expresses strong agreement, contradicting Susan's explanation
  • "You aren't sure about that" - suggests Cathy is uncertain, which isn't indicated in her statement
  • "I don't think it's a good idea" - clearly shows disagreement with Cathy's plan, which matches Susan's explanation
  • "I guess you're right" - expresses agreement, contradicting Susan's explanation
"I don't think it's a good idea" correctly shows Susan's disagreement with Cathy's plan to eliminate carbs.

III. Choose the word (A, B, C or D) that best fits each space in the following passage.

How do you usually spend New Year's Eve? I normally just enjoy being at home with my family. At midnight, we always (17) _______ the television and watch the firework display which takes place in the city center. The display is always amazing, with hundreds of fireworks of all colors and shapes exploding in the sky. Last December 31st, my sister and I (18) _______ to go and see the display for ourselves.

17.

Correct answer: A. turn on

When talking about activating electronic devices like televisions, "turn on" is the most common and natural phrasal verb to use. While "switch on" is also correct, "turn on" is more frequently used with televisions in everyday English.

  • "turn on the television" - natural, common expression
  • "switch on the television" - correct but less common
  • "put on the television" - this usually refers to playing specific content, not activating the device
  • "open the television" - not a standard expression for activating a TV

18.

Correct answer: A. decided

The context describes what the author and their sister did last New Year's Eve. "Decided" is the most appropriate verb as it indicates they made a choice to go see the fireworks display in person.

  • "decided to go" - correctly indicates making a choice or plan
  • "suggested to go" - would indicate merely proposing the idea, not necessarily acting on it
  • "agreed to go" - would imply someone else proposed the idea and they accepted
  • "offered to go" - would suggest proposing to do something for someone else
Since the text indicates they actually went to see the display, "decided" is the most appropriate choice.
